## 1. Emerging issues in global patent prosecution, portfolio management, and the impact of AI on prosecution

**Framing for this audience**: Topic 1 is **global** prosecution and portfolio management—**not** “what USPTO published last.” Federal Circuit judges are well supplied on **U.S. law**; they benefit from **what foreign systems require in practice** (specification culture, office guidelines, inventorship administration, PCT/national-phase pressure) and how **AI accelerates** those cross-border frictions. Use **Section 2** for deep dives on eligibility/support; here, stress **systems, incentives, and filing-office behavior**.

### 1.1 Cross-border infrastructure and why AI magnifies coordination cost

- **PCT and national-phase reality**: one specification serving **EPO-style technical-effect narrative**, **JPO-style support examples**, **CNIPA-style problem/solution and program-product claims**, and common-law written-description pressure—**claim churn** and **supplemental data** still arise from **business and prosecution choices**, while **AI-related prior art** and **faster public technical disclosure** in the field raise **global** novelty and obviousness pressure.
- **Prior art and disclosure volume**: faster publication pipelines, more machine-generated technical disclosure, multilingual prior art—**search and prosecution strategy** become global earlier in the life of a portfolio.
- **Multilateral dialogue** (non-binding but shapes expectations): WIPO and IP-office cooperation on AI-related IP issues; comparative studies (e.g., **JPO–CNIPA** work on AI-related inventions) as signals of **where examination is converging or diverging**.

### 1.2 Non-U.S. prosecution levers (what to bring to the room)

- **EPO**: *Guidelines* updates on CII/AI, sufficiency and technical effect, and how applicants **prove** effect without handing over the entire training stack—drives global spec and claim architecture for many multinationals.
- **JPO**: examination **case examples** for AI (eligibility, support/description, inventive step, clarity)—practical “if you draft like *this*, Tokyo reacts like *that*.”
- **CNIPA**: **Patent Examination Guidelines (2023)** changes for program products and AI/big-data inventions; **trial AI invention application guide (2024)** as an explicit typology of filings—useful for **how China sorts** algorithm vs. application vs. AI-assisted invention.
- **UKIPO**: relationship between **Patents Act 1977** exclusions and **technical contribution**; guidelines **in motion** with **litigation** (*Emotional Perception* line of cases)—a different dialogue than § 101 but equally important for **UK national and validation strategy**.
- **Regional EU angle (portfolio context)**: **EU AI Act** and product governance as **commercial/regulatory** pressure alongside **UPC + Unitary Patent** (enforcement side links to Section 3)—not a substitute for patent eligibility doctrine but part of **where to spend global IP budget**.

### 1.3 Inventorship, naming, and integrity of the record (international patchwork)

- Most major systems still assume **natural-person** inventors; **how** offices and courts expect applicants to **document human contribution** when AI tools are used in development or drafting **varies administratively** and in case law—worth describing ** comparatively**, not only through a U.S. lens.
- **Corporate practice**: who signs what, when, in **which jurisdiction**; consistency across priority applications and later national filings when AI use is disclosed or inferred.

### 1.4 Portfolio management themes (sector-agnostic)

- **Single R&D stream → divergent national rights**: when to **harmonize** vs. **fork** claim sets; divisionals/continuations in some systems vs. narrow first filings elsewhere.
- **AI tools on the prosecution side**: used well, they help counsel **analyze source code and large technical dumps more accurately**, produce **clearer diagrams** and structured descriptions, and **speed drafting**—without dictating **when** the enterprise files (still **business- and counsel-gated**) or replacing **counsel-driven** embodiment and use-case development.

## 2. How different jurisdictions treat AI-related patents: eligibility, written description, and enablement (comparative)

### 2.1 Europe (EPO): computer-implemented inventions and emerging sufficiency practice

- **Patentable subject matter**: claims to mathematical methods or “AI” per se vs. applications producing a technical effect in a technical field (G‑II, 3.3 of the Guidelines).
- **Inventive step**: formulation of the objective technical problem; technical effect of a trained model or specific implementation.
- **Sufficiency / disclosure (Article 83 EPC)**: plausibility of technical effect; what must be disclosed about training data, architecture, or parameters when the effect depends on them—2024 Guidelines emphasize proof short of exhaustive disclosure but caution against “invitation to a research programme.”
- **Key decisional anchor**: Enlarged Board approach to simulation/computer-implemented inventions—useful for analogizing ML/AI claim sets.

### 2.2 United Kingdom

- **Excluded subject matter**: programs for a computer “as such” under the Patents Act 1977; overlap and tension with EPO-style “technical contribution” analysis.
- **Recent litigation**: artificial neural networks and whether particular claim formulations fall within the exclusion—watch appellate and any further Supreme Court treatment for prosecution drafting lessons.

### 2.3 Japan (JPO)

- **Examination case examples** (eligibility, support/description, inventive step, clarity) as de facto guidance for international applicants.
- **March 2024 additions**: examples spanning LLM-related workflows, materials informatics, and model deployment—useful for comparing “description requirement” expectations to US written description.

### 2.4 China (CNIPA)

## 3. Emergence of the Unified Patent Court (UPC) and impact on European patent strategy

### 3.0 UPC primer — if you had never heard the acronym

Think in **U.S. rough analogies** (imperfect but orienting): the **EPO** examines and **grants** a single **European patent application**, but historically that grant became a **bundle of separate national patents** (“validation” in each country). **Infringement and validity** of those national slices were litigated **per country**—like needing **different district courts in many states** for the same patent family. That was expensive, slow, and strategically uneven.

**What changed.** Participating **EU member states** (not necessarily every EU country—**the UK is out** of this court; it is not a “European federal court” for all of Europe) created:

1. **Unified Patent Court (UPC)** — a **specialized patent judiciary** with **central, regional, and local** divisions. It can hear **infringement and revocation** for patents within its jurisdiction, with effect across **those participating states** in one proceeding.

2. **European patent with unitary effect** (commonly called **Unitary Patent** or **UP**) — **one** right that covers the participating states as a **single** territory, enforced/attacked **before the UPC** (subject to transitional rules).

**Classic European patent (non-unitary)** — still exists: after grant you can validate **nationally** as before. For those **classic EPs**, during a **transitional period** (years; verify current end date near your panel), the owner can **opt out** of the UPC so **only national courts** hear disputes—preserving the **old** fragmentation on purpose (e.g., to avoid one-shot central revocation, or to use familiar national courts).

**Why prosecution lawyers care** even if they “don’t litigate”: (i) **filing strategy** at grant—request **UP** or not; (ii) **opt-out** decisions on existing EPs; (iii) **claim clarity and prosecution history** because a **single** court may read the patent for a **large** economic territory; (iv) coordination with **EPO opposition** (central attack on validity at the EPO) vs **UPC revocation**—different timing, standards, and players.

**Timeline anchor.** The UPC **opened** **June 1, 2023**—still young; **early case law** and statistics are what practitioners watch. By **April 2026** (your panel date), you will want **updated** filing stats, a **few** headline decisions, and current thinking on **injunctions** and **forum** choice.

**Your panel**: **Benjamin Ruckert** is the natural **deep dive** on UPC procedure and German/European practice; your role can be **“how U.S. and in-house counsel decide whether to opt in to UP, opt out, or stay national”** and **how that interacts with global AI/software portfolios**.

### 3.1 Institutional basics for non‑EU practitioners

- **Unitary Patent + UPC**: single enforcement and revocation forum for participating EU member states vs. traditional European bundle + national courts.
- **Opt-out**: strategic choice for classic European patents, with implications for litigation risk and parallel national proceedings.

### 3.2 First years of operation (themes for clients)

- **Case volume and geography**: concentration of filings in certain local/regional divisions; language of proceedings (English vs. local language) affecting cost and speed.
- **Provisional measures**: standards for preliminary injunctions and evidence preservation compared to key national traditions (e.g., German practice).
- **Substantive patent law**: early claim construction, validity, and FRAND/SEP-adjacent issues as the body of reasoned decisions grows.

### 3.3 Portfolio management takeaways

- **Where to litigate**: UPC vs. national court vs. EPO opposition; settlement dynamics and injunction leverage.
- **Drafting and prosecution**: clarity and prosecution history estoppel-style considerations in a new court with developing doctrine.

## 4. Increased trade secret enforcement and interaction with patent strategy

### 4.1 Federal framework (United States)

- **Defend Trade Secrets Act**: civil federal cause of action, extraterritorial reach in defined circumstances, remedies (damages, injunctions, fee shifting in exceptional cases), and seizure provisions.
- **Pleading and identification**: circuit splits and district-level practice on how specifically a trade secret must be identified at pleading vs. through discovery—practical effect on litigation cost and risk.

### 4.2 How AI changes the patent vs. trade secret balance

The **classical factors** still matter: ease of **reverse engineering**, whether use is **detectable**, desired **term**, need for a **public fence** (patent) vs. **opacity**, and **foreign** enforceability. AI does not replace that framework—it **reweights** it.

- **Where the value lives**: for many ML systems, the durable edge is **weights, training data, curation, pipelines, and reward/feature engineering**, not the abstract idea. **Patenting** often forces **detailed technical disclosure** to satisfy enablement/support norms; if claims are still vulnerable to **eligibility** attacks, the firm may bear **disclosure cost** without strong patent scope—tilting the balance toward **trade secrecy** for the core stack while patents cover **surrounding** apparatus, integration, or specific deployable embodiments (a **layered** strategy).

- **Disclosure cost vs. scope**: broad **functionally claimed** AI may be hard to defend and **teaches**; narrow **implementation** claims may be easier to patent but **less** central to the business. That gap is where **TS + selective patent** mixes are negotiated.

- **Leakage surface area**: **remote work**, **API-delivered models**, **fine-tuning** with customer data, and **third-party AI vendors** complicate **reasonable secrecy measures** and chain-of-custody. Trade-secret **enforcement** becomes more operationally relevant even when patents exist on paper—**misappropriation** risk rises with digital mobility (feeds “enforcement feels up” in §4.3).

- **Counsel tooling (ties to §1.4)**: AI-assisted **code and document analysis** can improve **inventories** of what is (i) patentable without destroying TS, (ii) must stay secret, and (iii) already exposed—making the balance **more informed**, not automatically “patent less” or “patent more.”

- **Transparency and regulatory overlay**: **EU AI Act**–style **documentation, logging, and human-oversight** expectations can **tension** pure secrecy for certain deployable systems; **regulatory** artifacts may affect what can plausibly be claimed as confidential **know-how** in some markets.

- **Global asymmetry**: **Trade-secret** protection and **criminal/administrative** routes vary by country; a **patent** may still be rational in **weak-TS** jurisdictions or for **license clarity** even when the U.S. entity keeps weights as TS.

### 4.3 Why enforcement feels “up” even when patent filings shift

- **Complement or substitute**: protecting model weights, training pipelines, and data curation as trade secrets when patent protection is uncertain under § 101 or abroad.
- **Employee mobility and remote work**: higher incidence of misappropriation allegations; intersection with noncompetes and state UTSA variations.

### 4.4 Cross-border considerations (high level)

- **Criminal and administrative channels** in some jurisdictions vs. US civil DTSA path.
- **Choice of law and forum** in international supply chains.

## 6. Shifting U.S. landscape for patents and impact on global strategy

### 6.1 Judicial doctrine (Federal Circuit and Supreme Court shadows)
